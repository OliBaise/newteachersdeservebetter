New Teachers Deserve Better

We looked at data from the National Education Association and MIT’s Living Wage Calculator to work out whether teachers’ starting salaries can cover the cost of living in different Metropolitan Statistical Areas in the USA.

Key Findings

  • New teacher salaries only cover the cost of living for a single person in seven of the one hundred biggest Metropolitan Statistical Areas in the USA.

  • New teachers need an average 22.37% pay rise for their salary to match their cost of living. That equates to a net annual pay rise of $6,533.

  • New teachers can only cover their cost of living in 17 states in the USA.

  • New teachers in Denver need a 63% pay rise in order to match the city’s cost of living. This is the highest in the whole of the USA.

As we can see from these findings, only seven of the one hundred biggest Metropolitan Statistical Areas in the USA have starting teachers salaries that cover the cost of living for a single person.

They are:

  • Albuquerque, New Mexico ($5,126 annual discretionary income)
  • Bakersfield, California ($3,230 annual discretionary income)
  • Camden, New Jersey ($69 annual discretionary income)
  • El Paso, Texas ($2,072 annual discretionary income)
  • Fresno, California ($5,403 annual discretionary income)
  • Gary, Indiana ($1,638 annual discretionary income)
  • Washington-Arlington-Alexandria, DC-VA-MD-WV (MSAD) ($1,226 annual discretionary income)

On average, teachers need a net pay increase of $6,533 in order to match their cost of living.

Steve Nash, the General Manager of Wingman, who conducted this study, said:

“According to the Department of Education, there are currently 55,000 K-12 teaching vacancies in the USA and 270,000 teaching positions currently filled by underqualified staff.

This problem will remain unresolved unless new teachers are given salaries that cover their essential needs like rent, food and transportation.

Teaching is consistently ranked as one of the most stressful jobs someone can have, so teachers should not have to use their paid time off to take on second jobs in order to make ends meet”.

Methodology

In order to determine whether teachers’ starting salary can cover their cost of living, New Teachers Deserve Better looked at state by state starting teacher pay from the National Association of Educators.

They ran all of these through Paycheck City’s State Tax Calculator to deduct state taxes.

They then combined this with cost of living data from Massachusetts Institute of Technology’s Living Wage Calculator to find out whether first time teacher salaries in a specific state could cover the cost of living in the largest metropolitan areas in that state.

There is one limitation to the study.

We could only find reliable new teacher salary data on a state by state basis, rather than on a school district by school district basis. School districts only gave us overall teacher salary data. Since teacher salaries vary more by state and experience than by specific school district, we decided to use state salary data for new teachers specifically.

This means that more affordable metropolitan areas in comparably expensive states might have overinflated salary data.
